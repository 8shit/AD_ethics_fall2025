[Addictive Intelligence: Understanding Psychological, Legal, and Technical Dimensions of AI Companionship — Robert Mahari & Pat Pataranutaporn](https://mit-serc.pubpub.org/pub/iopjyxcx/release/2?readingCollection=132bb7af)  

---

## Summary  
This case study examines the hidden environmental cost of artificial intelligence, focusing on how data centers, hardware production, and resource extraction contribute to climate change, water scarcity, and electronic waste. While AI is often presented as a “virtual” and clean technology, the reality is that it depends on massive physical infrastructure with a growing carbon and ecological footprint. The article challenges the idea that technological progress is automatically sustainable and forces us to confront how digital convenience is tied directly to environmental degradation.

---

## Discussion 
What unsettled me most about this case study is how disconnected everyday AI use feels from the physical destruction behind it. I use a phone and a computer every day, and I only replace them every four to five years, which already feels moderate compared to many people. Yet even with that restraint, it’s genuinely frightening to realize that these devices rely on mining rare earth metals, factory labor, and energy-intensive manufacturing processes that permanently damage ecosystems. What makes it worse is that most of this technology is designed to be disposable. Devices are not built to be upgraded part by part; they are built to be replaced entirely. Even the idea of recycling feels hollow, since much of today’s electronic waste cannot be fully recycled and instead gets exported to poorer regions where it pollutes land, water, and air.

The environmental burden doesn’t stop at hardware. Data centers, which power everything from cloud storage to generative AI models, consume enormous amounts of electricity and water. Some large AI models require megawatt-hours of energy just for training, and data centers already account for a significant and growing share of global electricity use. What frustrates me most is that environmental planning often comes after these projects are built, not before. Companies establish massive server farms first, then later attempt to “optimize” them to be greener once the damage is already done. It feels backwards. Sustainability becomes a public relations strategy rather than a foundational design principle.

What creates a constant internal conflict for me is that, as a consumer, I don’t feel like I truly have a choice. I rely on technology for school, work, communication, and staying socially relevant. If I fall too far behind the upgrade cycle, I risk being cut off professionally and socially. So while I strongly care about the environment and genuinely fear where this trajectory is heading, I still participate in the same system that causes the damage. That contradiction is exhausting. It’s not that consumers don’t care; it’s that individual restraint does very little when the entire market is built around forced obsolescence and constant replacement.

This is why I place most of the responsibility on corporations and governments, not individuals. Corporations are profit-driven by design, and their incentive is to sell faster, upgrade faster, and produce more , regardless of environmental cost. Governments, on the other hand, have the power to regulate, yet too often they fail to act due to corruption, corporate lobbying, and personal political gain. Environmental regulations always seem to lag behind technological growth. We talk about climate change constantly, yet real enforcement on tech manufacturing, data center emissions, and electronic waste remains weak.

Even the idea of slowing down consumption is complicated by social hierarchy. If everyone were forced onto the same upgrade cycle, most people would adapt without issue. But in reality, technology has become a status symbol. New devices signal wealth, relevance, and success. That hierarchy pushes people to upgrade not out of necessity, but out of comparison. Environmental harm becomes an invisible side effect of social competition.

Looking forward, I realistically don’t see global tech consumption shrinking anytime soon. If anything, AI will increase the demand for hardware, servers, cooling systems, and network infrastructure. The environmental cost will likely grow alongside it unless sustainability becomes a legal requirement rather than a voluntary corporate promise.

## New Discussion Question
If environmental destruction is already built into the design of modern technology, is it realistic to expect consumers to fix the problem through “responsible use,” or does meaningful change only become possible when corporations and governments are forced to redesign the system itself?

## Reflection
This case study made me confront how easy it is to care about the environment in theory while still benefiting daily from systems that actively harm it. I genuinely believe in climate responsibility, yet I still depend on the very technologies that accelerate environmental damage. That doesn’t make me indifferent , it makes me frustrated. The fear comes from knowing that the destruction is real, measurable, and ongoing, while solutions remain slow, optional, and politically fragile.

What bothers me most is not that technology exists, but that it is built without environmental accountability from the start. We don’t design for longevity, repairability, or true recycling. We design for speed, scale, and replacement. By the time sustainability is even discussed, the infrastructure is already locked in.

This reflection pushed me to realize that environmental harm in tech is not a side effect , it is built into the business model. Until profits are no longer tied to endless production and replacement, and until governments enforce real environmental limits on the tech industry, individual awareness alone will never be enough. We will remain trapped between caring about the planet and needing the very tools that are helping to destroy it.
